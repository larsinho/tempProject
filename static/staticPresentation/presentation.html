Sequential Investment in Emerging Technologies under Policy Uncertainty

By Lars H. Sendstad and Michail Chronopoulos

www.sendstad.com/presentationInvestExL2nd

EU Guidance for Subsidies

  • Financial support for renewables should be limited and aim to make renewables competitive
  • Support schemes should be flexible and respond to falling production costs
  • Unannounced or retroactive changes to support schemes should be avoided

Objectives

  • How does policy uncertainty impact an investment decision?
  • How does technological uncertainty interact with policy uncertainty?
  • At what price should a firm adopt new versions of a technology under price, policy, and technological uncertainty?

Related work: Policy Uncertainty

  • Boomsma & Linnerud (2015)
    • Optimal investment when the current support scheme will be terminated or revised
    • The prospect of subsidy retraction increases the rate of investment if it is applied to new projects, while it slows down investment if it has a retroactive effect

Related work: Policy Uncertainty

  • Adkins & Paxson (2015)
    • Considers a fixed percentage premium and several policy interventions
    • Price, quantity, and policy uncertainty
    • Their results indicate that the prospect of a permanent subsidy retraction (provision) facilitates (postpones) investment
  • Yet they do not consider technological uncertainty

Related work: Technological Uncertainty

  • Grenadier and Weiss (1997)
    • Sequential investment
    • How does the innovation rate impact adoption strategy?
    • Leapfrog, laggard or compulsive?

Related work: Technological Uncertainty

  • Chronopoulos and Siddiqui (2015)
    • Sequential investment
    • Technological uncertainty can lead to early adoption
  • Yet they do not consider policy uncertainty

Model

  • Benchmark model with technological uncertainty

Assumptions: Policy Uncertainty

  • We consider both the sudden provision and retraction of a subsidy
  • The time between each policy intervention is exponentially distributed
  • Implies that there is an equal probability of a policy intervention conditional on the current time
    • \( \lambda_p dt\)
  • Example, \( \lambda_p = 0.2\) implies that the expected time until policy intervention is \(\frac{1}{0.2}=5 \) years

Assumptions: Technological Uncertainty

  • Technological innovations might arrive at any time
  • The new technology covers greater demand \( (D_{2}\geq D_1) \), yet at a higher cost \( (I_{2}\geq I_1) \)
  • Assumed to be uncorrelated with policy uncertainty and the price process
    • \( \lambda_\tau dt\)

Assumptions: Price Uncertainty

  • The output price, \(E_t\), follows a geometric Brownian motion (GBM)
    • \( dE_t = \mu E_t dt + \sigma E_t dZ_t, \quad E_0 \equiv E >0 \)
    • \(\mu\): the annual growth rate
    • \(\sigma\): the annual volatility
    • \(dZ_t\): the increment of the standard Brownian motion
  • By setting \(\sigma = 0\) we get the solution \(e^{\mu t } \), i.e. continously compunded interest rate
  • Electricity prices are often said to be mean reverting, but GBM with low growth rate is quite accurate for a long horizon (inflation? )

Benchmark Model: Operating second Technology

  • We use dynamic programming to solve the investment problem (start at the end)
  • The expected value of the revenues from operating the second technology
    • \( \Phi_{2,a}^{0,0}(E)=\frac{D_2E\left(1+ay\right)}{\rho-\mu}\)
  • The subsidy is fixed percentage on top of the output price

Benchmark Model: Before upgrade

  • In state (1,2) the firm receives the cash flows from operating the first technology, and has an embedded option to adopt the second one
    • \( \begin{align*} F_{2,a}^{0,0}(E)= & D_1E\left(1+ya\right)dt \\ & +\left(1-\rho dt\right)\mathbb{E}_E \left[F_{2,a}^{0,0}(E+dE)\right] \end{align*} \)
  • By expanding the right-hand side using Itô's lemma and solving the resulting ordinary differential equation, we obtain
    • \( \begin{eqnarray*}\label{eq44.8} F_{2,a}^{0,0}(E)= \begin{cases} \frac{D_1 E(1+ay)}{\rho-\mu}+A_{2,a}^{0,0}E^{\beta_1}&, E <\varepsilon_{2,a}^{0,0} \\ \Phi_{2,a}^{0,0}(E)-I_2 &, E\geq\varepsilon_{2,a}^{0,0} \\ \end{cases} \end{eqnarray*} \)

Benchmark Model: Technological Uncertainty

  • In state (1), the decisionmaker is operating the first technology version, and holds an option to invest in the second one that has yet to arrive
    • \( \begin{align*} \Phi_{1,a}^{0,0}(E) = D_1E\left(1+ay\right)dt +& \left(1-\rho dt\right)\bigg\{\lambda_\tau dt \mathbb{E}_E \left[F_{2,a}^{0,0}(E+dE)\right] \nonumber \\ & \ \ + \left(1- \lambda_\tau dt \right)\mathbb{E}_E \left[\Phi_{1,a}^{0,0}(E+dE)\right] \bigg\} \end{align*} \)
  • The expression of the value function in state (1)
    • \( \begin{eqnarray*}\label{eq44.11} \Phi_{1,a}^{0,0}(E)= \begin{cases} \frac{D_1 E\left(1+ay\right)}{\rho-\mu}+A_{2,a}^{0,0}E^{\beta_1}+A_{1,a}^{0,0}E^{\delta_1} &, \ E < \varepsilon_{2,a}^{0,0} \\ \frac{\left(\lambda_{\tau}D_2+\left(\rho-\mu\right)D_1\right)E\left(1+ay\right)}{\left(\rho-\mu\right)\left(\rho-\mu +\lambda_\tau \right)}-\frac{\lambda_{\tau}I_2}{\rho+\lambda_\tau} +B_{1,a}^{0,0}E^{\delta_2} &, \ E \geq \varepsilon_{2,a}^{0,0} \end{cases} \end{eqnarray*} \)

Benchmark Model: First Investment

  • Finally, the option to invest in the first technology version with an embedded option to invest in the second one is
    • \( \begin{align*} F_{1,a}^{0,0}(E)=(1-\rho dt)\mathbb{E}_E [F_{1,a}^{0,0}(E+dE)] \end{align*} \)
  • By expanding the right-hand side using Itô's lemma and solving the resulting ordinary differential equation, we obtain
    • \( \begin{eqnarray*}\label{eq44.12} F_{1,a}^{0,0}(E)= \begin{cases} C_{1,a}^{0,0}E^{\beta_1} &, E <\varepsilon_{1,a}^{0,0} \\ \Phi_{1,a}^{0,0}(E) -I_1 &, E\geq \varepsilon_{1,a}^{0,0} \end{cases} \end{eqnarray*} \)

What is the Benchmark Model?

  • Model with sequential investment
  • Random arrival of a technology
  • More representative for industries without heavy government interventions
  • No policy uncertainty

Permanent Subsidy Retraction

  • The expected revenue from operating the second technology version under a retractable subsidy is
    • \( \begin{eqnarray*} \Phi_{2,1}^{0,1}(E) = \frac{D_2E}{\rho-\mu} + \frac{D_2Ey}{\rho-\mu+\lambda_p} \end{eqnarray*} \)
  • In state (1,2) the decisionmaker is operating the first technology version and holds an option to invest in the second one
    • \( \begin{align*} F_{2,1}^{0,1}(E)= & D_1E(1+y)dt +\left(1-\rho dt\right)\biggl\{ \\ & \lambda_p dt \mathbb{E}_E \left[F_{2,0}^{0,0}(E+dE)\right] +\left(1- \lambda_p dt \right)\mathbb{E}_E \left[F_{2,1}^{0,1}(E+dE)\right] \biggr\} \end{align*} \)
  • Using Itô's lemma we obtain the expression of the value function in state (1,2)
    • \( \begin{eqnarray*}\label{eq45.5} F_{2,1}^{0,1}(E)= \begin{cases} \frac{ D_1 E}{\rho-\mu}+\frac{D_1Ey}{\rho-\mu+\lambda_{p}} +A_{2,0}^{0,0}E^{\beta_1}+A_{2,1}^{0,1}E^{\eta_1}&, E <\varepsilon_{2,1}^{0,1} \\ \Phi_{2,1}^{0,1}(E) -I_2 &, E\geq\varepsilon_{2,1}^{0,1} \\ \end{cases} \end{eqnarray*} \)

Permanent Subsidy Retraction: Technological uncertainty

  • We can combine the probability of a policy internvention with technological uncertainty as below
    • \( \begin{align*} \Phi_{1,1}^{0,1}(E)= & D_1E(1+y) dt+ \left(1-\rho dt\right)\Bigg\{ \\ & {\lambda_p dt} \bigg( {\lambda_\tau dt} \mathbb{E}_E \left[F_{2,0}^{0,0}(E+dE)\right] + {\left(1- \lambda_\tau dt \right)} \mathbb{E}_E\left[\Phi_{1,0}^{0,0}(E+dE)\right]\bigg) \\ & + {\left(1-\lambda_pdt\right)}\bigg( {\lambda_\tau dt} \mathbb{E}_E \left[F_{2,1}^{0,1}(E+dE)\right] + {\left(1- \lambda_\tau dt \right)}\mathbb{E}_E \left[\Phi_{1,1}^{0,1}(E+dE)\right]\bigg)\Bigg\} \nonumber \end{align*} \)
  • Next, by using similar steps as for the Benchmark model we can find the value function and investment threshold in the first technology

What is this model?

  • Model with sequential investment
  • Random arrival of technologies
  • Combined with a probability of a policy retraction
    • We can now investigate how policy uncertainty impacts the investment decision
    • How technological uncertainty interacts with policy uncertainty
    • The optimal adoption strategy under price, policy, and technological uncertainty

Results: Second Investment, Retraction

  • The investment threshold in the second technology
  • The prospect of a sudden subsidy retraction decreases the investment incentive
  • Price uncertainty increases the value of waiting

Results: First Investment, Retraction

  • The investment threshold in the first technology
  • Technological uncertainty mitigates the impact of policy uncertainty

Results: Second Investment, Provision

  • If we carry out the same analysis assuming that a subsidy might be suddenly provided
  • The investment threshold in the second technology
  • The prospect of a sudden subsidy retraction decreases the investment incentive
  • Price uncertainty increases the value of waiting

Results: First Investment, Provision

  • The option to upgrade to a more efficient technology further induces investment

Results: Provision of a retractable subsidy

  • An increase in \( \lambda_p \) implies that the extra value due the provision of the subsidy is reduced due to the likelihood of a subsequent subsidy retraction

Results: Infinite Provisions and Retractions

  • Infinite provisions and retractions mitigate the impact observed in the case of permanent provision/retraction.

Conclusion

  • The possibility of a sudden subsidy retraction decreases the incentive to invest
    • Decreased investment activity in Eastern Europe is thus expected as we have observed in Spain
  • Sudden provision of a subsidy hastens investment because the investment opportunity is more valuable
  • The possibility of sequential investment may mitigate or amplify the impact of policy uncertainty
    • Investing at a lower price in the current technology may position the firm for new technologies
    • Can be more important than a policy retraction
